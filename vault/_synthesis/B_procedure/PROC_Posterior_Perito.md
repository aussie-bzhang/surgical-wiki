## Procedure Summary
The Posterior Perito procedure involves the surgical resection of posterior peritoneal tumors or cysts, often requiring additional procedures such as hemicolectomy, cholecystectomy, or appendectomy. The standard indication is the diagnosis of a posterior peritoneal mass or cyst, which may be associated with significant involvement of adjacent structures.

## Case Volume & Context
A total of 5 cases were analyzed, representing a small but significant proportion of the total subspecialty workload for posterior peritoneal surgeries.

## Operative Approach
The dominant approach across cases was laparotomy, with variations documented including laparoscopic assistance in selected cases.

## Reconstruction Method
Anastomosis and reconstruction types included colonic resection with end-to-end anastomosis, biliary reconstruction with Roux-en-Y anastomosis, and appendectomy with primary closure. The frequency of each type was documented in the cases.

## Intraoperative Findings
Common intraoperative findings included the extent of tumor or cyst involvement, presence of adhesions, and anatomical variations. Disease patterns were predominantly localized to the posterior peritoneal space.

## Technical Highlights
Key technical points included meticulous dissection to preserve adjacent structures, careful handling of anastomoses to prevent leaks, and the use of adjuvant imaging for preoperative planning.

## Combined Procedures
Combined procedures included right hemicolectomy, cholecystectomy, and appendectomy, performed concurrently with the primary posterior peritoneal resection.

## Teaching Points
- **Point 1:** The importance of preoperative imaging for accurate tumor localization and planning.
- **Point 2:** The need for meticulous dissection to minimize the risk of injury to adjacent structures.
- **Point 3:** The significance of postoperative surveillance for early detection of recurrence.

## Complication Avoidance
Specific to this procedure, complications were minimized by careful anastomotic technique, thorough hemostasis, and postoperative monitoring for signs of infection or anastomotic leak.
