## Procedure Summary
Right hemicolectomy is a surgical procedure involving the removal of the right half of the colon, including the cecum, ascending colon, and the first part of the transverse colon. It is commonly performed for conditions such as colon cancer, diverticulitis, and inflammatory bowel disease.

## Case Volume & Context
A total of 24 cases were analyzed, representing a significant proportion of the subspecialty workload for general surgery.

## Operative Approach
The dominant approach across cases was open surgery, with laparoscopic techniques being utilized in select cases. Variations included the use of hand-sewn anastomoses and stapling devices.

## Reconstruction Method
Anastomosis was primarily performed using a coloileal anastomosis, with variations including ileotransverse anastomosis and colocolic anastomosis depending on the extent of the disease and patient-specific factors.

## Intraoperative Findings
Common intraoperative findings included the presence of tumor, adhesions, and anatomical variations. Disease patterns varied from localized to advanced, with some cases showing significant involvement of adjacent structures.

## Technical Highlights
Technical points that recurred across multiple cases included meticulous dissection of the mesocolon, careful vascular control, and precise anastomotic techniques to minimize complications.

## Combined Procedures
Combined procedures included cholecystectomy, nephrectomy, and adnexectomy, reflecting the multifaceted nature of some patient cases.

## Teaching Points
- The importance of thorough preoperative planning and understanding of anatomical variations.
- The significance of meticulous dissection and vascular control to minimize complications.
- The selection of appropriate anastomotic technique based on patient-specific factors.

## Complication Avoidance
Specific strategies to avoid complications included careful adhesion management, meticulous anastomotic technique, and postoperative monitoring for early detection of complications.
