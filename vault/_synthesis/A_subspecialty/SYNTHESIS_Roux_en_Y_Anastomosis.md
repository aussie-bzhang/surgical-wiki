## Common Intraoperative Findings
- Anatomical variations in the biliary tract
- Extensive adhesions requiring extensive dissection
- Presence of stones in the bile ducts
- Biliary strictures
- Gastric outlet obstruction

## Preferred Techniques
- End-to-side anastomosis for bile duct to jejunum
- Use of running sutures for anastomosis
- Ligation of the bile duct with a purse-string suture prior to anastomosis
- Adequate mobilization of the jejunum for anastomosis

## Technical Pearls
- Ensure adequate mobilization of the jejunum to avoid tension on the anastomosis
- Perform a meticulous dissection to avoid injury to adjacent structures
- Use a bougie to facilitate the passage of the bile duct during anastomosis
- Perform a thorough inspection of the anastomosis site for leaks or tension

## Complication Patterns
- Anastomotic leaks
- Postoperative bile leaks
- Biliary stricture
- Postoperative infection
- Anastomotic stenosis

## Teaching Points
1. The importance of meticulous dissection to avoid injury to adjacent structures.
2. The need for adequate mobilization of the jejunum to avoid tension on the anastomosis.
3. The significance of a thorough inspection of the anastomosis site for leaks or tension.
4. The management of complications such as anastomotic leaks and bile leaks.
5. The role of endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) in the management of biliary strictures.

## Knowledge Gaps
- The long-term outcomes of Roux-en-Y anastomosis in various clinical scenarios.
- The impact of anatomical variations on the success of Roux-en-Y anastomosis.
- The optimal timing and technique for the management of biliary strictures after Roux-en-Y anastomosis.
