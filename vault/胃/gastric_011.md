## Overview
Palliative total gastrectomy with esophagojejunostomy Roux-en-Y, spleenectomy, pancreatic tail resection, and right inguinal hernia repair for gastric cancer with peritoneal metastasis.

## Diagnosis
**Pre-operative:** Gastric cancer
**Intraoperative:** Gastric cancer with peritoneal metastasis
**Pathological:** Low-differentiated adenocarcinoma with tissue infiltration

## Intraoperative Findings
Intraoperative findings include 300ml of pale serous ascites, infiltration of the entire stomach, gastrocolic ligament, and splenocolic ligament, which could not be separated due to tumor infiltration. Lymph nodes in groups 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, 11, and 12 were enlarged and partially fused into masses. No significant abnormalities were found in the liver or pelvis. A right inguinal hernia was also noted with a hernia sac of 3*2cm, contents retracted, hernia ring diameter 1.0cm, located lateral to the inferior epigastric artery. Blood loss was 600ml, with 800ml of RBCs and 1000ml of plasma transfused.

## Operative Steps
1. Position the patient in supine position, under general anesthesia, and perform routine disinfection and draping. Make a midline upper abdominal incision to enter the abdomen.
2. Excise mesenteric nodes and send for pathology.
3. Separate the gastrocolic ligament, extending to the right to the hepatic flexure of the colon and to the inferior pole of the spleen. Strip the anterior leaf of the transverse mesocolon to the surface of the pancreas, excise the involved transverse mesocolon, suture and close the mesenteric defect, and open the lesser omentum to enter the lesser curvature side.
4. At the root of the pylorus under the pancreatic head, transect and ligate the right gastric artery, and clear the surrounding lymph nodes.
5. Transect the lesser omentum below the liver, extending to the hepatoduodenal ligament on the right, and clear the corresponding lymph tissue, transect and ligate the right gastric artery.
6. At 3cm from the pylorus, transect the duodenum with a side-to-side stapler and suture it for reinforcement.
7. Transect the splenic colic, splenogastric, splenorenal, and splenodiaphragmatic ligaments, free the splenic pedicle, separate the involved pancreatic tail and transect it, ligate the splenic artery and vein, and suture the pancreatic stump.
8. Continue to separate along the greater curvature to the left of the cardia of the stomach.
9. Flip up the gastric body, transect and ligate the left gastric artery at the root, and separate along the lesser omentum to the lower segment of the esophagus. Transect the esophagus at 3cm above the cardia, remove the specimen, and place the esophagus into a 25mm tube-shaped stapler anvil and fix with purse-string suture.
10. Transect the jejunum at 20cm from the Treitz ligament, and use a 25mm stapler to anastomose the esophagus with the distal jejunum (anterograde). Close the jejunal stump with a side-to-side stapler. Anastomose the proximal jejunum with the distal jejunum at 45cm below the esophageal anastomosis; close the mesenteric gap.
11. Thoroughly hemostasis, irrigate the abdomen, aspirate the effluent fluid, place two negative pressure drainage tubes at the anastomosis and duodenal stump, verify instruments and sponges, and close the abdomen in layers.
12. Perform routine disinfection and draping in the right inguinal area.
13. Make a right oblique incision along the inner and outer inguinal rings, and sequentially open the skin, subcutaneous tissue, and external oblique aponeurosis.
14. Incise the cremaster muscle, free the spermatic cord and protect it, find and free the hernia sac to the neck, ligate it high without opening the sac; open the transversalis fascia, expose the pectineal ligament.
15. Fix the mesh posterior to the spermatic cord, the lowest suture fixed to the pubic tubercle, below sutured with the pectineal ligament and inguinal ligament, above sutured with the conjoint tendon and inferior margin of the arcuate ligament, reshaping the internal ring, allowing the tip of a finger to pass.
16. Thoroughly hemostasis, and sequentially close the external oblique aponeurosis, subcutaneous tissue, and skin.
17. Complete the operation.

## Anatomical Key Points
> Critical structures: stomach, spleen, pancreas, esophagus, jejunum, inguinal canal. (胆总管)
> Danger zones: areas around the pancreas, splenic hilum, and inguinal canal.
> Structures requiring protection: splenic vessels, bile duct, and nerves.

## Technical Challenges
Difficulties encountered included extensive tumor infiltration, which made dissection challenging. Adequate hemostasis and anastomotic technique were critical.

## Teaching Points
- The importance of careful dissection around the pancreas and spleen to avoid injury to critical structures.
- The technique of esophagojejunostomy Roux-en-Y anastomosis.
- The management of a large inguinal hernia with mesh repair.

## Complication Prevention
- Adequate hemostasis to prevent postoperative hemorrhage.
- Proper anastomotic technique to prevent anastomotic leakage.
- Early detection and management of postoperative complications.
