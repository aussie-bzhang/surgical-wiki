## Overview
Gastrectomy for gastric cancer with esophagojejunostomy and jejunostomy. Involves resection of the entire stomach, followed by anastomosis of the esophagus to the distal jejunum in a Roux-en-Y fashion, and creation of a jejunostomy. Key technical feature is the meticulous dissection and anastomotic technique.

## Diagnosis
**Pre-operative:** Gastric cancer
**Intraoperative:** Gastric cancer
**Pathological:** Not documented

## Intraoperative Findings
Grossly, the tumor was located on the lesser curvature of the stomach, infiltrating the full thickness of the gastric wall. The wall was thickened and rigid, with the lesion extending from the cardia to the pyloric canal, causing complete deformation and obstruction of the pyloric canal. Enlarged lymph nodes were visible on the lesser curvature.

## Operative Steps
1. Supine position, under general anesthesia, routine disinfection and draping. Median upper abdominal incision for abdominal access.
2. Dissection of the gastrocolic ligament, extending to the right to the hepatic flexure of the colon and to the inferior pole of the spleen. The anterior leaf of the transverse mesocolon was stripped to the surface of the pancreas. The lesser omentum was opened to access the lesser curvature.
3. The right gastroepiploic artery was transected and ligated at the root of the pancreatic head, with lymph node dissection around it.
4. The lesser omentum was transected below the liver, extending to the hepatoduodenal ligament, with transection and ligation of the right gastric artery.
5. At 3 cm from the pylorus, the duodenum was transected and closed with a 60mm clamp and sutured for reinforcement.
6. The left gastroepiploic and short gastric vessels were transected along the greater curvature to the left of the cardia.
7. The stomach body was flipped up, the left gastric artery at the root was transected and doubly ligated. The lesser omentum was separated upwards, the esophageal hiatus and the parietal peritoneum were incised on both sides, the distal esophagus was freed, and the vagus nerves were transected. The esophagus was transected 3 cm above the cardia.
8. The jejunum was transected 20 cm from the Treitz ligament, and the esophagus was anastomosed to the distal jejunum with a 25.5 mm circular stapler. The jejunal stump was closed with a 60mm clamp.
9. The proximal jejunum was anastomosed to the distal jejunum 50 cm below the esophageal anastomosis (in front of the colon). The mesenteric gap was closed, and a jejunostomy was performed.
10. Hemostasis, peritoneal lavage, aspiration of effluent, placement of two negative pressure drainage tubes above and below the anastomosis, and careful counting of instruments and sponges. The abdomen was closed in layers.

## Technical Challenges
Difficulties encountered included meticulous dissection around the pancreas and duodenum, ensuring adequate lymph node dissection, and precise anastomotic technique.

## Teaching Points
- The importance of careful dissection around the pancreas and duodenum to avoid injury to these structures.
- The technique of creating a Roux-en-Y anastomosis with precise stapling and suturing.
- The management of lymph node dissection to ensure adequate staging.

## Complication Prevention
- Adequate hemostasis to prevent postoperative hemorrhage.
- Proper anastomotic technique to prevent anastomotic leakage.
- Adequate lavage of the abdominal cavity to reduce the risk of infection.
