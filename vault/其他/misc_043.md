## Overview
Diaphragmatic hernia repair with visceral reduction and fixation, abdominal wall incision patch tension suture, and left indirect inguinal hernia intraperitoneal repair in a patient with intestinal obstruction.

## Diagnosis
**Pre-operative:** Diaphragmatic hernia, visceral torsion and displacement, intestinal obstruction
**Intraoperative:** Confirmed diagnosis of diaphragmatic hernia, visceral torsion and displacement, intestinal obstruction
**Pathological:** Not documented

## Intraoperative Findings
Intraoperative findings include a 10*5cm diaphragmatic defect on the left side, most of the small intestine and right half of the colon herniated into the chest cavity, atrophy and atelectasis of the left lung. The spleen is twisted 720° around the splenic pedicle and displaced to the lower abdomen, presenting as a congestive splenomegaly. Multiple membranous adhesions are found in the abdominal cavity. Consultation with the thoracic surgery department was conducted, and the department agreed with the surgical approach. It was recommended to inflate the lung and place a chest drain in the left pleural cavity during anesthesia.

## Operative Steps
1. General anesthesia achieved, patient placed in supine position, routine disinfection and draping.
2. Midline upper abdominal incision around the umbilicus for abdominal exploration.
3. Suture the left inguinal canal orifice.
4. Locate the diaphragmatic defect, reduce the herniated contents.
5. Consultation with the thoracic surgery department, agreement with the department's approach, and recommendation for lung inflation and chest drain placement in the left pleural cavity during anesthesia.
6. Insert chest drain into the left pleural cavity, suture the defect to repair the diaphragmatic defect; a 12*8cm anti-adhesion patch is placed below the diaphragm to prevent rupture and sutured to the surrounding diaphragmatic tissue.
7. Decompress abdominal adhesions, reduce abdominal viscera, fix the pancreas and spleen to the upper left abdomen, and arrange the position of the small and large intestines.
8. Bleeding control, place two negative pressure tubes below the left diaphragm.
9. Due to reduced abdominal volume after visceral reduction, the excess anti-adhesion patch is used to repair the abdominal wall defect; the subcutaneous tissue and the anterior sheath of the rectus abdominis are freed 3.0cm along the abdominal incision, the patch is placed below the peritoneum, and sutured to the anterior sheath of the rectus abdominis.
10. Place a drain under the skin, suture the skin, and complete the operation.

## Anatomical Key Points
> Critical structures include the diaphragm, spleen, and abdominal viscera. The common bile duct (胆总管) is a structure requiring protection.

## Technical Challenges
Difficulties encountered include managing the large diaphragmatic defect and visceral torsion, ensuring proper reduction and fixation of abdominal viscera, and preventing postoperative complications.

## Teaching Points
- The importance of accurate diagnosis and preoperative planning
- The technique of diaphragmatic repair and visceral fixation
- The management of complex adhesions and visceral torsion

## Complication Prevention
- Proper surgical technique to minimize tissue trauma
- Adequate hemostasis to prevent postoperative hemorrhage
- Proper placement and management of drains to prevent infection and seroma formation
